ConocoPhillips Beats Estimates And Reiterates Intention To Spin-Off Downstream Operation

Jul. 27 2011 - 11:55 am | 1,208 views | 0 recommendations | 0 comments
By AGUSTINO FONTEVECCHIA

A ConocoPhillips refinery at South Killingholme - Wikipedia

ConocoPhillips reiterated its plan to spin off its downstream business in second quarter earnings, where the integrated oil and gas firm beat expectations on lower profits and falling production due to a protracted civil war in Libya.

In the second quarter, ConocoPhillips earned $3.4 billion, down from $4.2 billion a year ago in results that included a windfall from the sale of Lukoil.  Adjusted earnings per share were $2.41, up 57% from a year ago and well above Wall Street’s estimate of $2.19.

Global production reached 1.54 million barrels of oil equivalent (boe) per day, while refining capacity utilization rate stood at 91%.  ConocoPhillips is in the midst of a break up that will result in two separate companies, one for upstream and the other for downstream operations.

“The upstream company will be the largest U.S. pure-play E&P business, positioned for profitable growth from a rich resource base and a portfolio of quality investment opportunities,” read the release.  In the second quarter, the exploration and production (E&P) unit saw adjusted earnings increase by 72% to $2.6 billion, as ConocoPhillips capitalized on multi-year highs in oil prices. (Read Oil Prices: Brent-WTI Spread Above $22 And Here To Stay).

Production fell approximately 90,000 boe per day to 1.64 million as continued hostilities between Colonel Mummer al-Gadhafi and rebel forces essentially shut off operations in Libya.  Adjusting for Libya, “production exceeded levels from the second quarter of 2010 as new projects and lower downtime more than offset decline,” with production per share up 4% from a year ago.

Downstream operations had a slightly better quarter than in 2010, “primarily due to increased refining margins.”  Adjusted earnings were up 2.8% to $740 million.  U.S. refining capacity utilization rate hit 90% while the international utilization rate was an impressive 96%.

“We had a solid quarter,” said Jim Mulva, chairman and chief executive officer. “Higher adjusted earnings and cash flow were driven by better commodity prices and refining margins. Production performance was strong and capacity utilization of our refineries exceeded 90 percent.” During the second quarter, ConocoPhillips repurchased 42 million of its own shares, or 3 percent of shares outstanding, for $3.1 billion. This brings the company’s total shares repurchased to 9 percent of the shares outstanding at the inception of the repurchase program in 2010.

Shares in ConocoPhillips see-sawed during the day, opening with a nice pop on pre-market trading interest, then falling well below, only to rebound back into the green.  By 11:47 AM in New York, shares in Conoco were trading up 0.4% or 27 cents to $73.88.
