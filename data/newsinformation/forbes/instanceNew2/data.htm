    Surviving Transition: How To Keep The Family Business In The Family

    Northwestern MutualVoice Team, Northwestern Mutual
    comments, called-out

    If you’re part of a family business, you know the odds are stacked against long-term success. Only about a third of family businesses survive the transition to a second generation. Fewer still make it to a third generation. Family business failures can essentially be traced to one factor, according to the Family Business Institute—lack of succession planning.

    The typical family business owner spends a lifetime building the family business, but often overlooks the importance of planning for a successful transition to the next generation. Business owners are, understandably, focused on day-to-day operations, which often makes it hard for them to step back and plan for the future. Other business owners may purposely avoid succession planning because they have difficulty thinking about ‘letting go’ or just aren’t comfortable discussing money or inheritance issues openly.

    Whatever the reason, a majority of family businesses do not have a formal succession plan in place though the benefits are undeniable, according to Northwestern Mutual wealth management advisor Stephen Feehan, CFP®. “With a solid strategy in place, owners can ensure that their wishes for the future of the business will be honored,” said Feehan. “And, by establishing and funding a succession plan now, business owners can minimize eventual estate taxes tied to the business and maximize the wealth that’s passed on to the next generation.”

    Business life insurance is one often-overlooked option for funding the transfer of a business from one generation to the next. In upstate New York, second generation family-owned business Leonard Bus Sales is planning to use the proceeds from life insurance policies to transition the business to a third generation. “Having these policies in place takes a lot of weight off your shoulders,” said Jon Leonard, eldest son of current business president Mike Leonard. He sees three ways business life insurance will eventually help to protect what his father and grandfather worked so hard to build.

    • When Jon’s parents retire, the cash value of the permanent life insurance can be used as a funding mechanism for the next generation to buy them out.*
    • If Jon’s father dies before retiring, his mother would own the business. The life insurance death benefit could be used to fund her buyout.
    • If both parents pass away suddenly, the benefits could be used to pay estate taxes. Without those insurance proceeds, the children may be forced to raise a substantial amount of cash or potentially sell the business.

    “It’s comforting to know that these policies will help Leonard Bus Sales transition to the next generation seamlessly,” said Leonard. In addition to ensuring that the business stays in the family, Leonard says having business life insurance in place demonstrates to the community that the company is stable and secure. “We pride ourselves on being good partners—to our employees, our customers, our manufacturers and to our banks,” said Leonard. “There are a lot of stakeholders who rely on us to make the right decisions every day and this is one way to demonstrate that we care about the future of our business.”

    *Accessing the cash value of a permanent life insurance policy will reduce the available death benefit.
